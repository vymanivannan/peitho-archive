Pain and Relief Come of Themselves

Vyshali Manivannan

அரக்கர்

Arakkar (raksha)

Content note: evil eye and mouth, ritual dance

The Sinhalese Kolam ritual pays tribute to the rakshasas, the shapeshifting demon race that reigned over ancient Lanka, with folk dances using hand-carved, colorfully painted masks that represent the different forms rakshas can take. Like Naga Raksha (Cobra), adorned with numerous cobra hoods, a ferocious, sharp-toothed mouth and a slavering, protruding tongue, with the power to protect against many evil spirits. Or Gara Raksha (King of Demon), which — despite being smaller than some of other masks used in Sinhalese perahera (religious processions) — is widely used, brought out to conclude the dance or used in separate performances, where it acquires curative powers and dispels the influence of the evil eye and mouth. Valli's disabled Americanized son Kannan scoffs at this, but most Sri Lankans, including Valli, are true believers. She wants to bring Kannan back home to see such a dance, but Kannan refuses, citing his disbelief and, if not that, the difficulties of traveling internationally with chronic pain. The last time Valli returned to Sri Lanka, she visited a family friend she hadn't seen for 20 years, who gifts her three Raksha masks, saying, Hang them in a place he will always pass; when he visits you, they will work on him. As apotropaic artifacts, Raksha masks hang in many Sri Lankan houses. Valli secretly fancies that Kannan's health improves every time he comes home but has learned it's pointless to say this to a skeptic.
