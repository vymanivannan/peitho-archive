Pain and Relief Come of Themselves

Vyshali Manivannan

சக்கரம்

Chakkaram (wheel)

Content note: ethnic chauvinism

Kannan's Amma keeps calling this Kandyan brass plaque a wheel, only to backtrack and say, What wheel? I meant this round guy. Kannan understands this slippage, though: the brass circle plaque reminds him of the mud-engulfed chariot wheel where his mythical namesake Karna is killed. Every Sri Lankan Tamil he knows prefers Karna to Arjuna in The Mahabharata, and Ravana to Rama in The Ramayana. His family doesn't celebrate Diwali, though other Sri Lankan Tamils do, typically glossing over the victory of Rama over Ravana and focusing on the general triumph of good over evil. Kannan believes a homecoming is worthy of commemoration but sees no glory in Ravana's death or the razing of Lanka, or in Karna's beheading. Whenever Kannan visits home, he assesses his reflection in the wheel's highly polished surface, warped by its ridges and oxidized pattern. He takes heart in the similarities between his endurance of chronic pain and Karna's legendary stoicism, no matter that it resulted in his curse and, eventually, his divinely sanctioned death. As a people we prefer rakshasas and antiheroes. This is also an easy slippage. The gods play at their machinations because they fear our strength.
