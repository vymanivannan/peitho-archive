Pain and Relief Come of Themselves

Vyshali Manivannan

கரிக்கோல்

Karikkol (pencil)

Content note: magic, menstruation

The written word is a vessel of divinity, magic, and medicine. The Hindu tradition prizes literacy and education, so Valli is raised to revere all books, from The Ramayana to Christopher Pike, to treat them with respect. Don't drop them on the floor. Don't mar their pages. What spell might be cast, when a strikethrough or highlight materially alters a word? Book burning is a funeral pyre for tens of thousands of words, each one an entity with a soul (uyir, consonant) and body (mey, vowel). Writing utensils aren't treated with the same reverence, but when Valli is young, she is obsessed with the white Koh-i-Noor Rapidomatic mechanical drafting pencil her Appa owns for some reason. It's a drafting pencil for commercial artists and architects, and he doesn't belong to a design profession, but the recognizably South Asian Koh-i-Noor made him nostalgic enough to buy one. Mostly, he lets her play with it. She's especially fascinated by its knurled hardness indicator ring, which reveals cryptic numbers and letters neither of them understand: 2F, HB, 3B. As though the single piece of 0.5 lead she inserts is infinitely mutable, the words it forms changeling. When Valli gets her first period, her Amma — thankfully — doesn't give her a சாமத்திய வீடு (samathiya veedu), or age attainment ceremony, as she hated her own, but she does fondly remember receiving a Parker pen as a present that day. Valli is inspired to swipe the Koh-i-Noor for herself as a coming-of-age gift. However she understands the hardness indicators, whether she uses it to write or draw, it's a reminder of the quiet magic tucked into South Asian literacy.
