Pain and Relief Come of Themselves

Vyshali Manivannan

ஆமை

Aamai (tortoise)

Content note: corpse desecration, genocide, starvation

During her first postwar visit home, Valli travels to Monaragala district, where crocodiles were used to dispose of murdered Tamil corpses and coconut shell craft is still traditionally practiced. Her Western girlfriend, friends, and mentors all said, Bring me back something that reminds you of Sri Lanka. Before she escaped the conflict zone, Valli learned to save everything she could carry, in case it was needed. In her final days, she never let go of her half coconut shell, from which she could eat whatever little bit of கஞ்சி (kanji) — a thin red rice gruel, the only available sustenance in those nightmare days — she could obtain. When she fled, the shell stayed behind to sustain another. Today, coconut shellware and sculpture are her favorite Sri Lankan handcraft, especially the moving aamai, meaning tortoise or lazy person, because of her own frequent, inexplicable lassitude. A skilled coconut shell crafter can stretch a shell flat, many feet in length, manipulate it into shapes that transcend its original spherical form, combine it with other materials, polish it to look like ebony. The meat can be eaten, the water drunk, the shell transformed. Nothing is wasted. An object that saw so much death becomes a thing of beauty.
